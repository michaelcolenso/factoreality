## Chapter 1: Your Child Has Rights. Here's How to Use Them.

One parent paid $4,200 for a single IEP meeting. An advocate sat beside her, said the right words in the right order, and the school agreed to services they had been refusing for two years. When the meeting was over, the parent turned to the advocate and said: "I would have paid $100 for a book that told me what you just told me."

This is that book.

If you're reading this, you've probably sat in a meeting where everyone in the room seemed to know the rules except you. You nodded along. You signed things you didn't fully understand. You left with a vague sense that something wasn't right, but you didn't have the vocabulary to say what. You went home and spent the next three hours on Google, falling deeper into a tangle of acronyms — IEP, FAPE, LRE, PWN, IDEA — that seemed designed to be confusing.

That experience is not your fault.

The special education system is designed by professionals who speak its language every day. You speak it once or twice a year, in high-stakes meetings about the person you love most in the world. The information gap is real, and it has real consequences. Research consistently shows that children of parents who advocate effectively get more services. The law doesn't change based on who's asking — but outcomes do.

Schools are not universally adversarial. Most educators genuinely want to help your child. But schools operate under budget constraints, and resources go to the squeaky wheel. When families don't know what to ask for — and don't know they have the right to ask — those resources go elsewhere.

This book changes that.

**What the law says**

Two federal laws govern your child's right to support in US public schools. You don't need to become a lawyer to use them. You need to understand, at a practical level, what they require and what they protect.

The **Individuals with Disabilities Education Act (IDEA)** is the law that creates IEPs — Individualized Education Programs. First enacted in 1975 (as the Education for All Handicapped Children Act), IDEA has been reauthorized several times, most recently in 2004. It applies to children with one of 13 specific disability categories, including specific learning disabilities, autism, ADHD (under "other health impairment"), speech and language impairments, and others. Under IDEA, eligible children are entitled to a **Free Appropriate Public Education (FAPE)** — specialized instruction and related services, at no cost to the family, designed to meet their unique educational needs.

**Section 504 of the Rehabilitation Act of 1973** creates 504 Plans. Section 504 applies more broadly than IDEA — any child with a physical or mental impairment that substantially limits a major life activity qualifies. ADHD, anxiety, chronic illness, and depression routinely qualify under Section 504 even when they don't meet IDEA's more specific criteria. A 504 Plan provides accommodations and modifications (extended time on tests, preferential seating, modified homework assignments) but does not provide specialized instruction or the full procedural protections that IDEA offers.

**The scale of the issue**

You are not alone in navigating this. In the 2023–2024 school year, approximately **7.5 million children** — about 15% of all public school students in the United States — received special education services under IDEA. [U.S. Department of Education OSEP Fast Facts, 2024](https://sites.ed.gov/idea/osep-fast-facts-students-with-disabilities-2023-24/) Another study estimates that 1 in 5 children has a learning or attention issue of some kind. [National Center for Learning Disabilities, 2017](https://www.ncld.org) These are children in every school, every district, every state.

The law exists because Congress recognized, in 1975, that children with disabilities were being excluded from public education at staggering rates — some denied entry entirely, others warehoused in classrooms with no meaningful instruction. IDEA was a landmark civil rights law. Its protections exist specifically to prevent those outcomes from recurring.

**The long-term stakes**

The stakes are not just academic. Research shows that only 34% of adults with learning disabilities are employed full-time, compared to 60% of the general population. [National Center for Learning Disabilities, State of Learning Disabilities 2017](https://www.ncld.org) Children who receive appropriate early educational support — intervention that actually matches their needs, delivered consistently — have dramatically better outcomes as adults. They graduate at higher rates, attend post-secondary education at higher rates, and achieve greater economic independence.

When you advocate effectively for your child's IEP, you are not just fighting for a few extra sessions of speech therapy or a seat in a different reading group. You are affecting the trajectory of your child's life. That is not an exaggeration. The years when a child's brain is most plastic — when reading, language, and executive function skills are being laid down — are also the years when most of this advocacy happens. The window is real. What you do in it matters.

This isn't meant to put pressure on you. The system is hard to navigate and designed for professionals, not parents. What it means is that your effort — the time you're taking to read this book, to understand the process, to prepare for meetings — is not wasted. It is one of the most consequential investments you can make in your child's future.

**What this book will do for you**

This book walks you through the process in the order you'll encounter it. Chapter 2 helps you determine which plan — IEP or 504 — fits your child's needs. Chapters 3 and 4 cover the evaluation process and how to read an IEP document. Chapter 5 prepares you for the IEP meeting itself, including scripted responses to the pushbacks you're most likely to hear. Chapter 6 covers escalation — what to do when the school says no. Chapter 7 covers 504 Plans specifically. Chapter 8 gives you the records and documentation system that makes everything else work. Chapter 9 tells you what to do next.

At the end of each chapter, you'll find a short list of action items — specific things you can do this week.

**What this book won't do**

This is not legal representation. It is a practical guide written for parents who want to understand and use a system that was designed to serve their children. If your situation escalates to a formal due process hearing or litigation, you should work with a qualified special education attorney. This book will help you understand when that's necessary — and help you avoid reaching that point by getting things right earlier in the process.

Knowing the rules is the most powerful thing you can do. Let's get started.

## Chapter 2: IEP vs. 504 — Which Plan Does Your Child Need?

The most common question parents ask when their child is struggling is also the most important: does my child need an IEP or a 504 Plan? The answer depends on two factors: the nature of your child's disability, and how much support they need. This chapter gives you a framework for making that determination — including how to respond when the school has already decided and you disagree.

**The fundamental difference**

An **IEP (Individualized Education Program)** is created under IDEA. It is the more powerful of the two plans. An IEP provides:
- **Specialized instruction:** teaching methods and materials specifically designed for your child's disability
- **Related services:** speech-language therapy, occupational therapy, physical therapy, counseling, transportation, and others as needed
- **Annual measurable goals** with required progress monitoring and reporting to parents
- **The full procedural protections of IDEA**, including Prior Written Notice, independent evaluations, mediation, and due process

A **504 Plan** is created under Section 504 of the Rehabilitation Act. It provides:
- **Accommodations and modifications** in the general education classroom (extended time, preferential seating, breaks, modified testing format)
- **No specialized instruction** — the child receives the same curriculum and instruction as peers, just with adjustments to how they access it
- A faster, lighter process to obtain
- Fewer procedural protections than an IEP, but still legally enforceable through the Office for Civil Rights

Neither plan is better in the abstract. The right plan is the one that matches your child's actual needs.

**Who qualifies for an IEP**

To qualify for an IEP, your child must meet two criteria:
1. They must have a disability that falls into one of IDEA's **13 eligibility categories**
2. They must **need special education** — specialized instruction — as a result of that disability

IDEA's 13 eligibility categories are: autism, deaf-blindness, deafness, emotional disturbance, hearing impairment, intellectual disability, multiple disabilities, orthopedic impairment, other health impairment (which covers ADHD, asthma, epilepsy, and chronic health conditions), specific learning disability (dyslexia, dyscalculia, dysgraphia), speech or language impairment, traumatic brain injury, and visual impairment.

The second criterion — needing special education — is where many cases turn. A child with a learning disability may need specialized reading instruction (qualifies for an IEP). A different child with the same diagnosis who reads at grade level but struggles with organization may need accommodations but not specialized instruction (may be better served by a 504 Plan). The disability category is necessary but not sufficient; the child must also require specialized teaching.

**Who qualifies for a 504 Plan**

Section 504's eligibility standard is broader and does not require a specific disability category. Any physical or mental impairment that substantially limits one or more major life activities qualifies. Major life activities under Section 504 include: caring for oneself, performing manual tasks, seeing, hearing, eating, sleeping, walking, standing, lifting, bending, speaking, breathing, learning, reading, concentrating, thinking, communicating, and working.

Common conditions that qualify children for a 504 Plan include ADHD (the 2016 OCR Dear Colleague Letter explicitly confirmed this), anxiety disorder, depression, dyslexia when less severe, chronic illness (Type 1 diabetes, asthma, seizure disorders, Crohn's disease), and physical disabilities that don't require specialized instruction. A formal medical diagnosis is helpful but not always legally required; the school must evaluate the functional impact of the condition.

**A practical decision framework**

| Question | If Yes → | If No → |
|----------|---------|---------|
| Does your child's condition fall into one of IDEA's 13 categories? | Consider IEP evaluation | Consider 504 evaluation |
| Does your child need different teaching methods (not just different testing conditions)? | IEP likely needed | 504 may be sufficient |
| Is your child falling significantly below grade level despite current classroom support? | IEP evaluation warranted | 504 may be sufficient |
| Does your child need related services (speech, OT, counseling)? | IEP required | 504 may be sufficient |
| Is your child's primary need modifications to how they access an otherwise standard curriculum? | 504 may be the right fit | Consider IEP |

**A closer look at IDEA's 13 eligibility categories**

Understanding the categories helps you make the case for your child's eligibility — and anticipate the school's counter-arguments.

**Specific Learning Disability (SLD)** is the largest IDEA category. It covers dyslexia (reading), dyscalculia (math), dysgraphia (written expression), and related processing disorders. Schools use different models to identify SLD — a discrepancy between IQ and achievement, response-to-intervention data, or a patterns-of-strengths-and-weaknesses assessment. If the school says your child doesn't qualify, ask exactly which model they used, what data they relied on, and whether they assessed all areas of suspected disability.

**Other Health Impairment (OHI)** is the category that covers ADHD, as well as chronic conditions including epilepsy, Tourette syndrome, and Type 1 diabetes when those conditions result in limited alertness, vitality, or strength that adversely affects educational performance. Many children with ADHD qualify under OHI for an IEP — not just a 504 Plan — when the attention difficulties are severe enough to require structured specialized instruction, not just accommodations.

**Autism Spectrum Disorder (ASD)** covers a wide range, from minimally verbal children with significant support needs to children who are academically capable but struggle with communication and social pragmatics. Many autistic children need both specialized instruction and related services including speech-language therapy, occupational therapy, and social skills support.

**Emotional Disturbance (ED)** applies to children with significant, persistent emotional or behavioral difficulties — including anxiety disorders, depression, and conduct disorders — when those difficulties adversely affect educational performance. This category is frequently under-identified; schools sometimes treat behavior as a discipline problem rather than a disability requiring support.

**Speech or Language Impairment** covers articulation disorders, language processing difficulties, stuttering, and voice disorders. It is the second-largest IDEA category. Speech-language services are often the first door into special education for younger children whose broader learning disabilities haven't yet been formally identified.

**Why schools sometimes default to 504**

IEPs cost more to implement. They require specialized staff, structured services, formal progress monitoring, and extensive documentation. A 504 Plan can be created with less of all of these. Some schools — not all, but some — offer 504 Plans to children who might actually qualify for and benefit more from an IEP.

This is not always bad faith. Schools that are underfunded and over-stretched are making resource decisions that, unfortunately, do affect outcomes for individual children. Understanding the system means understanding this dynamic — and knowing how to respond.

If the school proposes a 504 Plan and you believe your child needs an IEP, you have the right to formally request an evaluation for special education eligibility under IDEA. That written request triggers the full IDEA process, including the 60-day evaluation timeline and all procedural protections. Chapter 3 covers exactly how to do this.

**The other direction: when a 504 is genuinely the better choice**

Not every battle is worth fighting for an IEP when a 504 would serve the child well. If your child's primary needs are accommodations — more time, a quiet room, digital materials, movement breaks — a 504 Plan can often be obtained faster and with less friction. It's not a lesser plan; it's the appropriate plan for a particular level of need.

The goal is getting your child what they need. Use the framework above to make that determination clearly, and know that you can revisit it — a child who starts with a 504 Plan can later be evaluated for an IEP if needs increase.

## Chapter 3: The Evaluation — How to Get Your Child Assessed (and What to Do If the School Says No)

Before any IEP or 504 Plan can be created, the school must evaluate your child. The evaluation determines whether your child has a qualifying disability and what their educational needs are. This chapter explains how to trigger that evaluation, what it must cover, and what to do if the school refuses or stalls.

**Child Find: the school's obligation to look first**

Most parents don't know this, but schools don't wait for parents to ask. Under IDEA's **Child Find** obligation, schools are required to identify, locate, and evaluate all children within their jurisdiction who may have disabilities that affect their education — including children who are performing adequately but whose needs are unmet. [20 U.S.C. § 1412(a)(3)]

Child Find applies from birth through age 21, across all public schools, charter schools, and some private programs receiving federal funds. "Wait and see" is not a legally valid strategy when a child is showing clear signs of a disability. If your child's teacher has been expressing concern for months and no evaluation has been initiated, the school may already be in violation of its Child Find obligations.

When you talk to the school, mention what you've observed at home. Teachers see children for a few hours a day. Parents have the full picture. Your observations are data.

**How to submit a written evaluation request**

The most powerful step you can take is submitting a written evaluation request. When you put the request in writing and the school responds with written consent — or when they agree and you sign their consent form — the **60-day clock begins**. [34 C.F.R. § 300.301]

Important: 60 days is the federal default. Many states have shorter timelines. California, for example, requires evaluation within 60 calendar days of consent. Texas uses 45 school days. Check your state's specific rules at [parentcenterhub.org](https://www.parentcenterhub.org).

Your written evaluation request should include:
- Your child's full name, grade, date of birth, and school
- A clear statement that you are requesting a comprehensive evaluation for special education eligibility under IDEA
- A description of the specific areas of concern (reading, math, attention, behavior, speech, motor skills — be specific)
- A request that the school confirm receipt of your request in writing and indicate when the evaluation process will begin
- Your signature and the date

Send this by email to both the principal and the special education director, and follow up by sending a physical copy by certified mail. Certified mail with return receipt creates a legal record that the letter was received on a specific date. That date matters for tracking the 60-day clock.

**What the evaluation must cover**

An IDEA evaluation is not just one test. It must assess your child in all areas related to the suspected disability using a variety of tools and strategies. [34 C.F.R. § 300.304] Depending on your child's needs, this may include:

- **Academic achievement testing** (reading fluency, reading comprehension, math calculation, written expression)
- **Cognitive assessment** (IQ testing, processing speed, working memory)
- **Language and communication** (expressive and receptive language, articulation)
- **Social-emotional and behavioral functioning** (behavioral rating scales, observations, interviews)
- **Adaptive behavior** (self-care skills, social skills, daily living skills — particularly relevant for autism)
- **Motor skills** (fine and gross motor, relevant for occupational therapy referrals)
- **Vision and hearing screening** (required by law before an initial evaluation)

The evaluation must use tools that are technically sound, administered by trained professionals, not culturally discriminatory, and given in the child's primary language. No single test can be the sole basis for an eligibility determination.

You have the right to receive a copy of the complete evaluation report before any IEP eligibility meeting takes place. Read it carefully. You are entitled to have it explained to you in language you understand.

**When the school evaluates but gets it wrong**

Sometimes the school conducts an evaluation and concludes your child doesn't qualify — or conducts an incomplete evaluation that misses areas of need. In both cases, you have the right to an **Independent Educational Evaluation (IEE)**.

Under 34 C.F.R. § 300.502, if you disagree with the school's evaluation, you can request an IEE conducted by a qualified professional outside the school district. When you request an IEE, the school must do one of two things:
1. Pay for the independent evaluation, or
2. Initiate a due process hearing to demonstrate that its own evaluation was appropriate

In practice, most districts choose to fund the IEE rather than go through due process. The IEE must meet the same standards as the school's evaluation, and the district must consider its results (though they don't have to accept all conclusions). One parent saved $2,800 in out-of-pocket evaluation costs by invoking this right — and the independent evaluation identified needs the school had missed, leading to a significantly stronger IEP. [COPAA parent survey](https://www.copaa.org)

**What to do if the school refuses to evaluate**

When the school refuses to evaluate your child — whether for initial eligibility or for additional areas you've requested — they must give you a **Prior Written Notice (PWN)** explaining why. This document is described in Chapter 5, but the essential point is: a refusal to evaluate must be in writing, must explain the reason, and must inform you of your procedural rights.

If you receive a refusal, your options include:
- Requesting an IEE (if they conducted a partial evaluation)
- Filing a state complaint alleging a violation of Child Find obligations
- Requesting mediation
- Initiating due process

All of these options are covered in Chapter 6. For now, the most important thing is: don't accept a verbal "no." If the school won't evaluate, get the refusal in writing and know that you have next steps.

**During the evaluation: your role as a parent**

You are not a bystander in the evaluation process. IDEA explicitly includes parents as part of the evaluation team. [34 C.F.R. § 300.305(a)] Here's how to make the most of that role:

*Provide your own input.* Most evaluators will ask you to complete behavioral rating scales and questionnaires. Fill these out carefully and honestly. If a questionnaire only allows "never / sometimes / often / always," attach a separate sheet describing specific behaviors you've observed — the concrete examples are more useful to evaluators than numerical checkboxes.

*Ask for a pre-evaluation meeting.* You can request a meeting with the evaluators before testing begins to ensure they understand your specific concerns and are planning to assess all relevant areas. If your child has a language processing issue, confirm that the evaluation will include language testing, not just IQ and achievement.

*Review the evaluation report before the eligibility meeting.* You have the right to receive the evaluation report a reasonable number of days before any IEP meeting where eligibility will be determined. If the school schedules the eligibility meeting for the same day as the evaluation, ask for a postponement. You need time to read the report, understand what the scores mean, and formulate your questions and responses.

*Bring your own documentation.* Medical records, private evaluations, therapy reports, teacher communications documenting ongoing struggles — all of this is information the team must consider. Compile it into a packet and give copies to each evaluator.

**After the evaluation: what the eligibility meeting will look like**

Once the evaluation is complete, the IEP team meets to determine eligibility. At this meeting, the evaluators will present their findings and the team will discuss whether your child meets IDEA criteria. You are a full member of this team.

Come to the eligibility meeting having read the report. Write down questions about any scores you don't understand. If the report says your child scored at the 8th percentile in phonological processing, ask: What does that mean in practical terms? How does it affect reading instruction? Does that score indicate a need for specialized teaching?

If the team determines your child is not eligible, request Prior Written Notice explaining the specific evaluation data and legal criteria that were used to reach that conclusion. This document is your starting point for any challenge.

## Chapter 4: Reading the IEP — What Every Section Actually Means

The IEP document can run 20 to 50 pages depending on the district's format. Parents frequently sign it in the meeting after reviewing it for perhaps 15 minutes. This chapter breaks down every major section so you know exactly what you're looking at, what to question, and what to insist on.

**Present Levels of Academic Achievement and Functional Performance (PLAAFP)**

This section — sometimes called "Present Levels" or "PLOP" in older documents — is the most important part of the entire IEP. It describes where your child is right now, academically and functionally. Everything else in the IEP should flow from and connect back to the PLAAFP.

A good PLAAFP includes specific, current data: "Maria reads connected text at a rate of 62 words per minute with 84% accuracy, which is below the expected 120 words per minute for a 4th-grade student. Her comprehension score on the most recent assessment was at the 12th percentile." That level of specificity gives you a real baseline.

What to look for:
- Are the data points current (from this school year or the most recent evaluation)?
- Are the scores specific and objective — not just "below grade level" but a specific measurement?
- Does the PLAAFP describe how the disability affects the child's participation in the general curriculum?
- Does each area of need described in the PLAAFP connect to at least one annual goal?

If the PLAAFP is vague, general, or based on observations from two years ago, push back on it. The quality of the PLAAFP directly determines the quality of the goals.

**Annual Goals**

IDEA requires that annual goals be measurable. [34 C.F.R. § 300.320(a)(2)] In practice, many school districts write goals that are technically in the IEP but would fail any honest standard of measurability.

A legally sufficient and practically useful goal answers five questions:
1. **Who?** (Your child, named)
2. **What will they do?** (The specific skill or behavior)
3. **Under what conditions?** (Given a 4th-grade passage; given a graphic organizer; with no verbal prompts)
4. **To what level?** (80% accuracy; 4 out of 5 trials; independently; in 3 consecutive sessions)
5. **By when?** (By the next annual IEP review; by February of the current school year)

**Example of a non-measurable goal:**
"Emma will improve her reading skills."

This goal tells you nothing. You cannot measure "improve." There's no baseline, no target, no timeline. It could be satisfied by any change — including changes that have nothing to do with the school's instruction.

**Example of a measurable, legally compliant goal:**
"Emma, given a passage at a 3rd-grade Lexile level, will correctly identify the main idea and two supporting details with 80% accuracy across four consecutive measurement opportunities, as measured by teacher-administered reading probes, by June 2026."

This goal is specific, observable, measurable, and tied to a timeline. Progress can be tracked. At the end of the year, you can know whether Emma achieved it.

If you review your child's IEP and the goals look like the first example, you can request that the team reconvene to rewrite them. This is a legitimate request and should not be characterized as being difficult — measurable goals are a legal requirement.

**Services**

The services section is where the school commits to what it will actually provide. Each service listed in the IEP should specify:
- The **type of service** (specialized reading instruction, speech-language therapy, occupational therapy, counseling, behavioral support)
- The **frequency and duration** (3 times per week for 30 minutes per session)
- The **location** (general education classroom, resource room, speech room, individual pull-out)
- The **start and end dates**
- The **provider's role** (though specific staff names are not required)

This section creates legal obligations. "60 minutes per week of specialized reading instruction in a small group of no more than 4 students" is a commitment. If your child is only receiving 30 minutes, or if the group has 10 students, or if the service is being canceled regularly, the school is not implementing the IEP — and that is an IDEA violation.

Keep a service log. Each week, note whether services were provided as written. If sessions are missed, note the date and reason. Patterns of missed services, documented over time, form the basis of a complaint or request for compensatory services.

**Progress monitoring: holding the school accountable between meetings**

The IEP must describe how the school will measure progress toward each annual goal and how and when parents will be informed of that progress. [34 C.F.R. § 300.320(a)(3)] Progress reports are typically provided quarterly — at the same intervals as report cards — and they should tell you whether your child is on track to meet each annual goal by the end of the year.

Read these progress reports carefully. A progress report that says only "making progress" or "goal partially met" is not informative enough to understand where your child actually stands. Push for specific data: "As of January 2026, Emma is reading at 75 correct words per minute with 90% accuracy, compared to a goal of 95 correct words per minute by June. She is progressing and is expected to meet the goal by year-end." That's a useful progress update.

If the progress reports show your child is not on track to meet goals with the current level of service, request an IEP meeting to discuss what changes are needed before year-end. You do not have to wait for the annual review. IDEA allows you to request an IEP meeting at any time. [34 C.F.R. § 300.324(a)(6)] Put the request in writing and keep a copy.

**Supplementary Aids and Services**

One section of the IEP that parents often overlook is Supplementary Aids and Services — the supports provided in general education settings to enable a child with a disability to participate alongside peers. [34 C.F.R. § 300.42]

These might include: a classroom paraprofessional (aide), modified or adapted materials, assistive technology (text-to-speech software, speech-to-text tools, FM hearing systems), preferential seating, extended time on classroom assignments (not just tests), modified grading procedures, or communication supports.

Supplementary aids and services are required to be considered before placing a child in a more restrictive setting, and they can make the difference between a child thriving in general education and being unnecessarily moved to a self-contained classroom. Ask explicitly at each IEP meeting: "What supplementary aids and services are we providing in the general education classroom, and are they sufficient?"

**Placement and Least Restrictive Environment (LRE)**

IDEA requires that children with disabilities be educated in the Least Restrictive Environment — meaning, to the maximum extent appropriate, alongside children without disabilities. [34 C.F.R. § 300.114] The law requires schools to consider a full continuum of placement options and to use supplementary aids and services to enable children to participate in general education before moving to more restrictive settings.

LRE is frequently misapplied in both directions. Some schools use LRE to justify keeping a child in a general education setting where they have no meaningful access to instruction — "she's included, that's LRE." Others use it to justify a more restrictive placement without providing the supports that would enable general education participation. The correct analysis is: can this child receive an appropriate education in a less restrictive setting with supplementary aids and services?

When reviewing the placement section, ask:
- What supplementary aids and services (classroom aide, modified materials, assistive technology) were considered before a more restrictive placement was proposed?
- Is there data showing that the proposed placement is the least restrictive that meets this child's needs — not just the most convenient?
- Does the placement allow for meaningful interaction with peers without disabilities?

**Signatures: what you're actually agreeing to**

IEP documents typically include multiple signature pages. Read carefully before signing any of them.

- **"Parent participated in the development of this IEP"** — You are signing that you were present and involved. This does not mean you agree with everything in the document.
- **"Parent consents to implementation of the IEP and the delivery of services"** — This is the actionable consent that allows services to begin. You can consent to some services and not others; document any partial consent clearly.
- **"Parent acknowledges receipt of procedural safeguards notice"** — Signing this only confirms you received the document, not that you waive any rights.

You are legally entitled to take the IEP home before signing. At the end of the meeting, request a copy and state that you will review it and return your consent within a few days. If any school staff member indicates you must sign before leaving, or that services cannot start until you sign at the meeting, write the following next to your signature: "Parent received copy for review; signature does not constitute agreement with content." Then follow up in writing within a few days with your actual consent — or your specific objections.

**Extended School Year (ESY) services**

Extended School Year services are special education and related services provided beyond the normal school year — typically during the summer. IDEA requires ESY services when the IEP team determines that a child needs them to receive a Free Appropriate Public Education. [34 C.F.R. § 300.106]

The trigger for ESY is usually "regression" — evidence that the child loses significant skills over breaks and requires a disproportionate amount of time to recoup them. Regression is measured by comparing the child's skill level at the end of the school year to their skill level at the beginning of the following year.

ESY eligibility must be determined individually — the school cannot have a blanket policy that no child receives ESY, or that ESY is only available to children in certain categories. If your child tends to lose ground over summer break and then spends the first two months of school catching up instead of advancing, request that the IEP team evaluate ESY eligibility. Put this request in writing and make it part of the annual IEP review discussion.

**Transition planning (for students age 14 and older)**

For students who are 16 or older (and in some states, beginning at 14), the IEP must include a **Transition Plan** — a coordinated set of activities designed to help the student move toward post-secondary education, vocational training, employment, independent living, and community participation. [IDEA, 20 U.S.C. § 1414(d)(1)(A)(i)(VIII)]

The transition plan must be based on the student's own strengths, preferences, and interests — not just what the school offers. It must include measurable post-secondary goals (e.g., "After high school, Jordan will attend a two-year community college to pursue a program in graphic design") and a list of transition services and activities.

Parents of teens with disabilities should begin thinking about transition well before age 16. What does your child want to do after high school? What skills are they developing or need to develop? What support will they need in adulthood? The IEP team must address these questions — and your child's voice is particularly important in this process.

## Chapter 5: The IEP Meeting — How to Walk In Prepared and Walk Out With What You Need

The IEP meeting is the most consequential event in your child's special education year. It's where teams set goals, commit to services, and make placement decisions that affect your child's entire next twelve months. Walking in prepared — with the right documents, the right people, and the right language — is the difference between an IEP that works and one that looks good on paper and delivers nothing.

**Who must be in the room**

IDEA defines the IEP team with specificity. Required members include: [34 C.F.R. § 300.321]

- **The parents** — you are a required team member, not a guest
- **At least one general education teacher** if the child participates in general education
- **At least one special education teacher or provider**
- **A school district representative** qualified to supervise special education and authorized to commit district resources
- **An individual who can interpret evaluation results** (often the same as the district rep)
- **The child**, when appropriate (strongly recommended for middle and high school; required for transition planning at age 16)
- **Other individuals at the parents' or school's discretion**, including specialists, outside therapists, and advocates

Schools may ask to excuse a required team member, citing scheduling conflicts. They can only do so with your **written consent in advance**. If you receive a notice that a required member won't attend, you have the right to postpone rather than proceed without them. If the person being excused has information relevant to the issues being discussed, it's reasonable to insist they be present.

**What to bring**

Arrive prepared. Bring:
- Copies of your child's previous IEPs and any evaluation reports you have
- Your own written observations of what's working, what isn't, and what you're asking for — organized by subject and setting
- Documentation of any services that weren't delivered as written (dates, what was missed, who you spoke to)
- A copy of the draft IEP if the school sent one ahead of time (they should, but many don't)
- A notepad for taking notes during the meeting — what is said, agreed to, and declined
- A recording device, if permitted. Most states allow one-party consent recording of conversations you're a party to. Look up your state's laws before the meeting.
- An advocate, family member, friend, or other support person of your choosing

**Bringing a support person**

You have the unconditional right to bring any person of your choosing to an IEP meeting. [34 C.F.R. § 300.321(a)(6)] This can be a spouse or partner, a parent advocate, a special education attorney, a neighbor who used to be a teacher, or your child's private therapist. The school has no authority to prohibit your guest. They may express surprise or mild pushback — that is not a legal objection. Simply say: "I've invited [Name] to support me as a member of the team. They're here in an advisory capacity."

Bringing a knowledgeable support person changes the meeting dynamic. It signals that you're informed and supported. It often results in the school being more careful about what commitments it makes — and keeping them.

**Reviewing the draft IEP before the meeting**

Some districts send a draft IEP to parents several days before the annual meeting. Many don't. If yours doesn't, request one — send an email to the special education coordinator a week before the meeting asking to receive the draft IEP in advance so you have time to review it.

When you review the draft IEP, go through each section using the Chapter 4 framework:
- Do the present levels include current, specific data?
- Are the annual goals measurable with clear conditions, criteria, and timelines?
- Does the services table specify frequency, duration, and location for each service?
- Is the placement appropriate, and has LRE been genuinely considered?
- Are there any services you requested that are missing?

Write your questions and concerns down before the meeting. If the draft includes services that weren't discussed at previous meetings, or omits services you believe are needed, note them specifically. Don't wait for the meeting to notice these things.

**Your right to record the meeting**

Recording an IEP meeting can be valuable — it removes any ambiguity about what was said and agreed to, and it can serve as evidence if commitments aren't honored. Your right to record depends on state law.

Most states follow the one-party consent rule: if you're a participant in the conversation, you can record without notifying the other parties. Some states require all-party consent. Check your state's recording consent laws before the meeting. Even in all-party consent states, you can notify the school at the start of the meeting that you intend to record — they may push back, but in most states they cannot prohibit you from recording as long as you're a party to the conversation.

If you record meetings, keep the recordings organized and labeled by date. Recordings are most useful when a dispute later arises about what was committed to — not as a weapon, but as a factual record.

**Preparing your own position paper**

Before the meeting, write a one-page summary of your observations and requests. It doesn't need to be formal. Include: what you've observed at home (specific behaviors, struggles, strengths), what your child says about school, what you're asking the team to do or change, and any services or supports you believe your child needs. Bring copies for everyone at the table. This creates a record that your perspective was formally presented, and it ensures you don't forget what you meant to say when you're in the room.

**Scripted responses to the 8 most common school pushbacks**

These scripts are designed to be calm, grounded, and specific. You don't need to cite exact regulation numbers. The goal is to demonstrate that you know your rights, that you're documenting the process, and that you intend to pursue the matter if it isn't resolved.

**Pushback 1: "We don't have the resources for that service."**
Your response: "I understand resources are a challenge. But IDEA requires the district to provide services that are appropriate for my child regardless of resource constraints — 'lack of resources' isn't a legal basis for denying a service. Can you note in the meeting record that resources were cited as the reason? I'll also need Prior Written Notice of this refusal."

**Pushback 2: "He's doing fine in the classroom."**
Your response: "The present levels in the IEP show he's performing at the [X]th percentile in [area]. I want to understand what data we're using to say he's 'doing fine,' because the evaluation data tells a different story."

**Pushback 3: "Let's revisit this at the next annual review."**
Your response: "I'd like to address this today. If the team decides not to make a change, I'd like that documented as a team decision with the rationale. And I'd like to know what data would need to change for us to revisit this before the annual review."

**Pushback 4: "A 504 is enough for your child."**
Your response: "I'd like to formally request an evaluation for special education eligibility under IDEA. I'd like to start that process today. Can you provide me with the consent form?"

**Pushback 5: "We're going to try this for 30 days and see."**
Your response: "I'm open to a monitoring period. I'd like that plan in writing with specific benchmarks — what data will be collected, over what period, and what outcome would lead to implementing the service. Can we document that today?"

**Pushback 6: "He doesn't qualify."**
Your response: "I'd like to see the specific evaluation criteria that weren't met, in writing. I'm also going to consider requesting an Independent Educational Evaluation, and I'll let you know within the next few days."

**Pushback 7: "You're overreacting."**
Your response: "I hear you, but I'd like to focus on what the data shows. Can we go through [specific evaluation result] together? I want to make sure I'm understanding it correctly."

**Pushback 8: "We're already doing our best."**
Your response: "I know the team works hard, and I appreciate it. I'm here to work with you. Let's go through the present levels section together and talk about where the gaps are between where my child is now and where they need to be."

**When to request an adjournment**

If new information is presented that you haven't had time to process, if you feel pressured to sign something immediately, or if the conversation becomes heated or unproductive, you have the right to request an adjournment.

Say: "I want to make sure I'm reviewing this carefully and giving it the attention it deserves. I'd like to take the draft IEP home and come back to finalize it. Can we schedule a follow-up meeting within the next two weeks?"

The school may push back, citing inconvenience or urgency. You are not obligated to sign anything in the meeting. If pressure is significant, say: "I appreciate the team's time. I'll have my response to you in writing within three business days." Then leave.

**After the meeting: the follow-up email**

Within 24 hours of the meeting, send a brief confirmation email to the special education coordinator: "Dear [Name], thank you for today's meeting. To confirm what was agreed to: [list specific decisions — services added, goals changed, timelines committed to]. Please let me know if anything is different from your notes." This creates a written record of commitments made. If the school's version of events is different from yours, you want to know immediately — not at the next annual review.

**When you can't agree: procedural options at the end of the meeting**

If the meeting ends without agreement — you disagree with a proposed service, goal, or placement, and the school is not moving — you have several options to take before you leave.

First, clearly state your disagreement on the record. Don't let the meeting end with everyone nodding ambiguously. Say: "I want to be clear that I do not agree with [specific element of the IEP]. I am documenting my objection and I'll be following up in writing." This ensures the disagreement exists in the meeting record and isn't treated as consent by silence.

Second, request Prior Written Notice. Whenever the school proposes to take an action (add a service, change a placement) or refuses to take an action you requested, they must provide you with a Prior Written Notice explaining the decision, the data it's based on, your procedural rights, and sources of support. [34 C.F.R. § 300.503] Request this document in writing at or immediately after the meeting. It becomes part of your records and is the starting document for any future escalation.

Third, you can sign the IEP with a written objection. You may choose to consent to services you agree with while formally noting your objection to specific elements. Write your objection clearly next to your signature, or attach a written statement. Consenting to what's agreed while documenting what isn't gives your child access to the services that were agreed on without implying you've accepted the disputed elements.

## Chapter 6: When the School Says No — How to Escalate Without Going to War

You have asked. You have followed up in writing. You have cited the law. And the school still says no. This chapter is about what comes next. Escalation does not mean litigation — and for most families, it never gets there. There is a ladder of options, and most disputes can be resolved well before the top rung.

**Step 1: Written request to school leadership**

Before escalating formally, send a written letter or email to the principal or special education director. Be specific: name the service or action the school is failing to provide, cite the IEP section or legal requirement it violates, and request a written response within 10 business days. Many compliance issues — services not being delivered as written, meeting timelines being missed — get resolved at this step when leadership realizes you're documenting.

Keep the tone factual and professional. State the facts. State what's required. State what you're asking for. Avoid language that personalizes the conflict or characterizes intent.

**Step 2: State complaint**

Every state has a state education agency (SEA) — your state's Department of Education — that oversees IDEA compliance. You can file a written complaint with the SEA alleging that the school district has violated IDEA. The SEA must investigate and issue a written decision within **60 calendar days** of receiving your complaint. [34 C.F.R. § 300.153]

A state complaint is the right tool when the school is clearly not following federal law — services written in the IEP aren't being delivered, evaluation timelines were violated, Prior Written Notice wasn't provided, or Child Find obligations were ignored. It's free to file, doesn't require an attorney, and creates a formal government record of the violation. If the SEA finds a violation, it must require the district to correct it and can order compensatory services.

What a state complaint can't do: it can't make the school provide services it hasn't already agreed to. It is a compliance tool, not a tool for arguing about what services are appropriate. For disputes about the appropriateness of services, mediation or due process are the right vehicles.

Find your state SEA's complaint process at [parentcenterhub.org](https://www.parentcenterhub.org).

**Step 3: Mediation**

IDEA requires every state to offer a voluntary mediation program. Mediation brings parents and school district representatives together with a trained, neutral mediator to try to reach a written agreement. [34 C.F.R. § 300.506]

Mediation is free for parents — states fund the mediator. It is voluntary for both parties, meaning the district can decline. It is confidential. It is typically faster than due process — most mediations conclude in one or two sessions over a few weeks.

Mediation works best when both parties have a genuine interest in reaching agreement but can't bridge the gap on their own. A skilled mediator helps each side understand the other's perspective and can surface creative solutions that a formal hearing wouldn't produce. Agreements reached in mediation are legally binding.

Mediation is less effective when the district is not negotiating in good faith, or when you need a formal legal ruling to compel compliance with a clear legal obligation.

**Step 4: Due Process**

A due process hearing is a formal legal proceeding before an impartial hearing officer. It is the most powerful — and most costly — option available to parents.

Due process can result in:
- A binding order requiring the district to provide specific services
- An award of compensatory education (services to make up for what was missed)
- Reimbursement for private special education services you paid for
- Payment of your attorneys' fees if you prevail

Due process is also:
- Time-consuming: resolution typically takes 6–18 months
- Expensive: families that prevail often spend $15,000–$50,000 in legal fees before reimbursement
- Damaging to relationships: your child will continue attending the same school after the hearing

Before initiating due process, consult a special education attorney. Many offer free initial consultations. COPAA's attorney directory is at [copaa.org](https://www.copaa.org).

**The comparison: state complaint vs. mediation vs. due process**

| | State Complaint | Mediation | Due Process |
|-|----------------|-----------|-------------|
| Cost to parent | Free | Free | $15k–$50k+ |
| Time to resolve | 60 days | Weeks | 6–18 months |
| Attorney required | No | No | Strongly recommended |
| School can refuse | No | Yes | No |
| Result is binding | Yes (if violation found) | Yes (if agreement reached) | Yes |
| Best for | Compliance violations | Relationship-based disputes | Denied services with significant harm |

**The escalation mindset: preserve what can be preserved**

Parents who have been through formal disputes consistently offer the same advice: separate the fight from the people. Your child's teacher sees your child every day. That relationship is worth protecting. Your dispute is with the system — with budget decisions, policy choices, and sometimes individual administrators. It is rarely with the teacher in the room.

In all written communications during escalation, keep your tone factual and non-accusatory. State what was required. State what happened instead. State what you need. Leave room for the other side to resolve the issue without losing face. This approach is both ethically sound and strategically effective.

**Compensatory education: making up for lost time**

When a school fails to deliver services that were written in the IEP — either because of a compliance failure or because the wrong program was provided for an extended period — you may be entitled to **compensatory education**: additional services to make up for what your child didn't receive.

Compensatory education is not automatic. You must request it and provide documentation of the missed services. The calculation is not always hour-for-hour; hearing officers and courts have discretion to award what is reasonably designed to remedy the educational harm. But compensatory services — extra tutoring, an extended IEP year, additional therapy sessions — are a real remedy available to families who have documented their children's missed services.

If you believe compensatory education is warranted, document the specific services missed (dates, type of service, what was provided instead), consult with your state PTI center or a special education attorney, and raise the issue formally in writing.

**The stay-put rule**

One important protection you should know: during any pending due process proceeding, your child has the right to remain in their current educational placement. [IDEA, 20 U.S.C. § 1415(j)] This is called the "stay-put" rule.

The stay-put rule prevents schools from changing a child's placement as a retaliatory response to parents challenging the IEP. It preserves the status quo while the dispute is being resolved. If the school proposes a placement change after you file for due process, you can invoke stay-put to keep your child in their current setting until the hearing is resolved.

## Chapter 7: 504 Plans — A Shorter Path to Classroom Accommodations

For children whose disability can be managed with changes to how they experience the classroom — more time on tests, a quieter workspace, access to recorded lectures, permission to take movement breaks — a 504 Plan may be the most direct and least contentious route to meaningful support.

**Eligibility: broader than you might think**

Section 504 requires only that a child have a physical or mental impairment that substantially limits one or more major life activities. The 2008 amendments to the Americans with Disabilities Act, which apply to Section 504 interpretation, explicitly broadened the definition of disability and made clear that the determination of whether an impairment substantially limits a major life activity should not be demanding.

ADHD nearly always qualifies. A 2016 Dear Colleague Letter from the Office for Civil Rights stated directly that "ADHD can substantially limit a major life activity" and that schools "have an obligation to evaluate a student when they suspect a disability." [OCR Dear Colleague Letter, July 2016](https://www2.ed.gov/about/offices/list/ocr/docs/dcl-504-adhd-201607.pdf) Anxiety disorders, depression, and chronic health conditions qualify routinely. If your child has any condition that affects how they learn, concentrate, or access the school environment, a 504 evaluation is likely warranted.

**Requesting a 504 evaluation**

Submit your request in writing to the school's principal or 504 coordinator. Your letter should describe the condition, how it affects your child's ability to access the curriculum or participate in school activities, and ask that the school conduct a 504 evaluation.

Unlike the IDEA evaluation process, there is no federal 60-day deadline for 504 evaluations. However, the school must act without "undue delay" — and most states have guidance on reasonable timelines. If the school takes more than four to six weeks without explanation, send a written follow-up.

**The evaluation process**

504 evaluations are typically lighter than IDEA evaluations. The school can draw on existing information: teacher observations, report cards, state test results, and medical documentation you provide. You can provide a letter from your child's pediatrician or psychologist describing the diagnosis and its educational impact. The school must consider this information; they are not required to accept it as determinative, but they must document their reasoning if they reject a medical provider's assessment.

**What a 504 Plan should contain**

Section 504 does not prescribe a specific format, but a well-constructed 504 Plan includes:
- The specific accommodations to be provided, written precisely enough to be enforceable
- The name or role of the staff member responsible for each accommodation
- How accommodations will be communicated to all of the child's teachers
- A mechanism for the child to notify a teacher if an accommodation isn't being provided
- A review schedule (at minimum annually)

Vague accommodation language creates compliance problems. "Extended time" should specify: extended time of 1.5x (or 2x) on all timed assessments, including in-class tests and standardized testing. "Preferential seating" should specify: a seat at or near the front of the classroom, away from high-traffic areas and windows.

**Common 504 accommodations**

| Category | Specific Examples |
|----------|------------------|
| Time | 1.5x or 2x extended time on tests; extended deadlines for long-term assignments; flexible pacing |
| Environment | Preferential seating; testing in a separate, quiet room; reduced visual distractions |
| Materials | Digital textbooks; text-to-speech software; graphic organizers; notes provided in advance |
| Testing format | Multiple-choice instead of written answers; oral responses; open-note assessments; chunked testing |
| Medical/physical | Scheduled bathroom breaks; access to water; snack breaks; access to nurse; designated quiet space |
| Behavioral/attention | Check-in/check-out with a trusted adult; scheduled movement breaks; fidget tools allowed; frequent feedback |

**Enforcing a 504 Plan**

504 Plans are enforced by the **Office for Civil Rights (OCR)** of the U.S. Department of Education — not through IDEA's due process system. This is different from IEP enforcement, and it matters.

If your child's 504 Plan accommodations aren't being implemented, document specifically what wasn't provided (which accommodations, which dates, which staff members). Attempt informal resolution with the school first. If the problem continues, file an OCR complaint at [ocrcomplaint.ed.gov](https://ocrcomplaint.ed.gov).

OCR complaints are free. OCR aims to resolve cases within 60 to 180 days. OCR investigations often result in Resolution Agreements that require the district to take specific corrective actions, train staff, and monitor compliance. For accommodation failures, this is often the fastest and most direct path to resolution.

**When to push for an IEP instead**

If your child's needs are growing beyond what accommodations can address, the 504 isn't enough. Consider requesting an IEP evaluation if:
- Your child continues to fall behind academically despite 504 accommodations being in place
- Accommodations alone don't address the need — your child needs different instruction, not just different testing conditions
- Your child needs related services (speech therapy, occupational therapy, counseling) that a 504 can't provide
- Your child is spending significant time outside the general classroom for academic reasons but without a formal IEP structure

If this describes your situation, return to Chapter 2 and the evaluation process in Chapter 3.

**Filing an OCR complaint: the step-by-step process**

If your child's 504 Plan is not being implemented — accommodations aren't being provided, the school is refusing to create a 504 Plan despite documented need, or the district is retaliating against you for advocating — the Office for Civil Rights complaint process is your direct enforcement path.

To file an OCR complaint:

1. **Document the violation.** Write out specifically what accommodation was required (as stated in the 504 Plan), what actually happened on which dates, and what impact the failure had on your child.

2. **Attempt informal resolution first.** Send a written complaint to the school principal and the district's 504 coordinator describing the specific failure and requesting correction within 10 business days. Retain a copy. In many cases this resolves the problem — schools that receive a formal written complaint recognize the next step is a government agency investigation.

3. **If informal resolution fails, file with OCR.** Go to [ocrcomplaint.ed.gov](https://ocrcomplaint.ed.gov) and complete the online form. You must file within 180 days of the discriminatory act (or within 60 days if you have a contractual grievance process with the school). Describe the violation, attach your documentation, and submit.

4. **OCR investigates.** OCR will notify the school of your complaint and conduct an investigation. OCR can dismiss, close with resolution, or find a violation. If OCR finds a violation, it typically negotiates a Resolution Agreement requiring the district to take corrective action, provide compensatory services, train staff, and report back to OCR on compliance.

5. **Monitor compliance.** If a Resolution Agreement is reached, track whether the district is following it. If it isn't, you can return to OCR.

## Chapter 8: Keeping Records, Writing Emails, and Creating a Paper Trail

The best advocate in any IEP meeting is also the best record-keeper. Documentation is not paranoia — it is the foundation of effective advocacy. Everything that matters in special education needs a written record. This chapter gives you the system.

**The everything-in-writing rule**

Verbal agreements in special education have one legal value: zero. If a principal tells you in a hallway conversation that they'll add speech services in October, that's a pleasant conversation. Until it's reflected in an IEP amendment with your signature, it is not a commitment the law will enforce.

This rule works both ways. When the school makes a commitment, confirm it in writing. When you make a request, make it in writing. When you disagree with something, document your disagreement in writing. The paper trail you build is the record of what was promised, what was denied, and what happened.

**The 24-hour follow-up email**

After any phone call or in-person conversation with school staff that involves a decision, commitment, or new information, send a brief email within 24 hours:

> "Hi [Name], thank you for speaking with me today about [Child's name]. I wanted to confirm what we discussed: [specific commitment or decision — who will do what, by when]. Please let me know if I've understood anything incorrectly. I look forward to [next step]."

This email does two things. First, it creates a dated written record of what was agreed to. Second, it gives the school an immediate opportunity to correct any misunderstanding. If they don't respond, the written record of what you confirmed stands. If they respond with a correction, you've avoided a future disagreement about what was said.

**Documenting service delivery (or non-delivery)**

If your child's IEP specifies services at a particular frequency and you suspect those services aren't being delivered, start a service log. For each week, note:
- Was the service scheduled to occur? (Yes/No)
- Did it occur? (Yes/No — or "Parent not notified")
- If it didn't occur: was there a reason given? Was it made up?

Ask your child. Ask the teacher. Ask the service provider directly: "Can you help me understand the schedule for [child's] speech sessions? I'm trying to keep track for my own records."

After two or three weeks of documented non-delivery, send a written inquiry to the special education coordinator. After a documented pattern, escalate per Chapter 6.

**What to keep in your child's file**

Every parent of a child with an IEP or 504 Plan should maintain a complete file. The items to collect and keep:
- Every IEP document — initial and all annual revisions
- Every IEP amendment
- Every evaluation report — initial and all re-evaluations
- Every Prior Written Notice received
- Every IEP meeting notice
- Progress reports (quarterly or semester)
- Every report card
- All email correspondence with school staff
- Notes from every phone call (date, time, who called, what was said)
- A copy of the procedural safeguards notice (schools must provide this annually)
- Samples of your child's dated schoolwork, especially when you see regression

**Requesting your child's complete educational records (FERPA)**

Under the Family Educational Rights and Privacy Act (FERPA), you have the right to inspect and receive copies of your child's complete educational records. [20 U.S.C. § 1232g] The school must respond to your request within 45 calendar days.

Submit this request in writing to the school principal or records coordinator. A simple letter or email stating: "I am requesting a complete copy of all educational records for [Child's full name], DOB [date], including but not limited to all evaluations, IEP documents, progress reports, teacher notes, disciplinary records, and any other documents in the educational file."

Schools are permitted to charge reasonable copying fees, though they cannot charge fees that effectively prevent you from exercising this right. Many districts will provide digital copies at no charge if you request it.

Certified mail creates a legal record of delivery. It also sends a signal: you're treating this as a legal matter. School administrators respond differently to certified mail than to email. One parent described the effect: "The moment I sent a certified letter citing IDEA, everything changed. The school started responding to my emails within 24 hours." **Free help in your state**

Every state in the US has a federally funded **Parent Training and Information (PTI) center**, required by IDEA. [20 U.S.C. § 1471] PTI centers are staffed by advocates who know your state's specific laws, timelines, and processes. They provide free services including:
- One-on-one guidance navigating the IEP or 504 process
- Help preparing for meetings and understanding evaluation reports
- Training workshops on parents' rights
- Accompaniment to IEP meetings in some cases (varies by state)
- Referrals to local attorneys and advocates

Find your state's PTI center at **[parentcenterhub.org](https://www.parentcenterhub.org)**. This is your single most important free resource. Call them before your next meeting.

[pacer.org](https://www.pacer.org)
The national parent advocacy center, based in Minnesota. PACER provides practical, plain-language guides for parents of children with disabilities; a parent-to-parent network that connects you with other parents who have navigated similar situations; and resources spanning first evaluations through transition planning for adulthood. PACER's "ACTion Sheet" series provides one-page summaries of key rights topics that are ideal for quick reference.

**Understood.org**
[understood.org](https://www.understood.org)
Built by a consortium of fifteen learning disability nonprofits, Understood is the strongest consumer-facing resource for parents who are newer to navigating learning differences. It is particularly good at explaining what evaluations assess, what different learning disabilities look like in the classroom, and what accommodations look like in practice. For parents who want to understand the "why" behind their child's diagnosis, Understood is an excellent starting point.

**Wrightslaw.com**
[wrightslaw.com](https://www.wrightslaw.com)
The comprehensive legal reference for special education advocacy. Pete and Pam Wright have published several books on IDEA and Section 504; the website includes their full text of federal regulations, case summaries, articles on every aspect of the IEP process, and a community forum. If you want to go deep on a specific legal question — what exactly does 34 C.F.R. § 300.502 require, how have courts interpreted "appropriate" in FAPE — Wrightslaw is where the answers are.

**Office for Civil Rights Complaint Portal**
[ocrcomplaint.ed.gov](https://ocrcomplaint.ed.gov)
For filing Section 504 complaints. Free, online, no attorney required. You'll need to describe the discriminatory act, when it occurred, and the school district involved. OCR will acknowledge your complaint within a few weeks and begin its evaluation process.

**When to hire a professional**

**A parent advocate (non-attorney):** When you need an experienced person to accompany you to meetings, review IEP documents, and help you prepare. Parent advocates typically charge $50–$200 per hour and are significantly less expensive than attorneys. Find one at copaa.org or through your state PTI center.

**A special education attorney:** When the school has denied significant services and informal resolution has failed, when you're considering due process, or when any legal proceeding is involved. Attorneys typically charge $200–$500 per hour. If you prevail in due process, attorneys' fees may be recoverable from the district. Find one at copaa.org or your state bar association's referral service.

**You can do a great deal of this yourself.** The evaluation process, annual IEP meetings, and most common disputes — including many involving service delivery failures — are navigable with the knowledge in this book and free support from your state PTI center. Save professional help for the situations where the stakes are high enough to warrant the cost.

**A final note**

You are the most important person on your child's IEP team. Not the district representative. Not the school psychologist. Not the principal. You.

You know your child in a way no professional in that room ever will. You see them before school when they're dreading the day. You see them after school when they're exhausted from working twice as hard as their classmates just to keep up. You are their constant advocate, their primary witness, and their most important expert.

The system exists to serve your child. It doesn't always do so without being held to that standard. Holding it to that standard is your job — and now you know how.

Use the templates. Call your PTI center. Come back to whatever chapter fits where you are. The rules haven't changed. What has changed is that you know them.

Your child's story isn't written yet. The next meeting, the next evaluation, the next IEP — those are all still ahead of you. Every parent who has fought this fight says the same thing: start earlier than you think you need to, document everything, and don't let anyone make you feel that asking for what your child is legally entitled to is asking for too much. It isn't. It is exactly what these laws were designed for.
