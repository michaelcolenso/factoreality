## How to Get Your Child the Support They're Legally Entitled To — Without Hiring a $5,000 Advocate

## Chapter 1: Your Child Has Rights. Here's How to Use Them.

One parent paid $4,200 for a single IEP meeting. An advocate sat beside her, said the right words in the right order, and the school agreed to services they had been refusing for two years. When the meeting was over, the parent turned to the advocate and said: "I would have paid $100 for a book that told me what you just told me."

This is that book.

If you're reading this, you've probably sat in a meeting where everyone seemed to know the rules except you. You nodded along. You signed things you didn't fully understand. You left with a vague sense that something wasn't right, but you didn't have the vocabulary to say what.

That experience is not your fault. The special education system is designed by professionals who speak its language every day. You speak it once a year, maybe twice, in high-stakes meetings about your child. The information gap is real, and schools benefit from it — not because every educator is acting in bad faith, but because limited resources go to the families who know how to ask for them.

The good news: the rules are public. They're federal law. And once you know them, you cannot un-know them.

**The two laws you need to know**

Two federal laws govern your child's right to support in public school:

The **Individuals with Disabilities Education Act (IDEA)** is the law that creates IEPs — Individualized Education Programs. It applies to children with one of 13 specific disability categories, including learning disabilities, autism, ADHD (under "other health impairment"), and others. Under IDEA, eligible children are entitled to specialized instruction and related services (like speech therapy or occupational therapy) at no cost to the family.

**Section 504 of the Rehabilitation Act of 1973** creates 504 Plans. Section 504 applies more broadly — any child with a physical or mental impairment that substantially limits a major life activity qualifies. ADHD, anxiety, and chronic illness routinely qualify under Section 504 even when they don't meet IDEA's more specific criteria. A 504 Plan provides accommodations (extended time, preferential seating, modified testing) but not specialized instruction.

Chapter 2 will help you figure out which law — and which plan — applies to your child. For now, the most important thing to understand is this: these are not suggestions. They are federal entitlements. Your child has a legal right to an appropriate education, and the school is legally required to provide it.

**What this book will do**

This book walks you through the process in the order you'll encounter it: understanding which plan applies, requesting an evaluation, reading the documents, preparing for meetings, handling school pushback, and escalating when necessary. Each chapter ends with action items you can do this week.

**What this book won't do**

This is not legal representation. If your situation involves a formal due process hearing, you should consult a special education attorney. This book will help you understand when that's necessary — and help you avoid reaching that point by getting things right earlier in the process.

In the United States, approximately 7.5 million children — about 15% of all public school students — receive special education services under IDEA. [U.S. Department of Education OSEP Fast Facts, 2024](https://sites.ed.gov/idea/osep-fast-facts-students-with-disabilities-2023-24/) Another estimated 1 in 5 children has a learning or attention issue. [National Center for Learning Disabilities, 2017](https://www.ncld.org) Your child is not the exception. You are not overreacting. And you have more power in this process than anyone has told you.

Let's start using it.

## Chapter 2: IEP vs. 504 — Which Plan Does Your Child Need?

The most common question parents ask when their child is struggling in school is also the most important one: "Does my child need an IEP or a 504 Plan?"

The answer depends on two things: what your child's condition is, and how much support they need. This chapter gives you a clear framework for making that determination — including what to do when the school has already decided and you disagree.

**The fundamental difference**

An **IEP (Individualized Education Program)** is created under IDEA. It provides your child with:
- Specialized instruction (teaching methods tailored to their disability)
- Related services (speech therapy, occupational therapy, counseling, etc.)
- Annual goals that must be measurable
- The full set of procedural protections under IDEA (more on those throughout this book)

A **504 Plan** is created under Section 504 of the Rehabilitation Act. It provides:
- Accommodations in the general education classroom (extra time, preferential seating, modified homework assignments, etc.)
- No specialized instruction
- A simpler, faster process to obtain
- Fewer procedural protections than an IEP, but still legally enforceable

**Who qualifies for an IEP**

To qualify for an IEP, your child must:
1. Have a disability that falls into one of IDEA's 13 eligibility categories, AND
2. Need special education (specialized instruction) as a result of that disability

The 13 IDEA categories include: autism, deaf-blindness, deafness, emotional disturbance, hearing impairment, intellectual disability, multiple disabilities, orthopedic impairment, other health impairment (which includes ADHD, asthma, and chronic illness), specific learning disability (which includes dyslexia and dyscalculia), speech or language impairment, traumatic brain injury, and visual impairment.

A diagnosis alone doesn't guarantee an IEP. The child must also need specialized instruction — not just accommodations — because of their disability. A child with ADHD who struggles to sit still but reads and performs at grade level might need accommodations (extended time, movement breaks) but not specialized reading instruction. That child may be better served by a 504 Plan.

**Who qualifies for a 504 Plan**

Section 504's eligibility standard is broader: any physical or mental impairment that substantially limits one or more major life activities. Major life activities include learning, reading, concentrating, thinking, communicating, and caring for oneself.

Conditions that commonly qualify for a 504 Plan include ADHD, anxiety disorder, depression, dyslexia (when not severe enough for an IEP), chronic illness, seizure disorders, and Type 1 diabetes. Note that a formal medical diagnosis helps but is not always legally required — the school must evaluate what the condition is and how it affects the child, regardless of what a doctor has or hasn't said.

**Practical decision framework**

| Question | Answer → |
|----------|----------|
| Does your child have a diagnosis in one of IDEA's 13 categories? | Yes → Consider IEP; No → Consider 504 |
| Does your child need specialized teaching methods (not just accommodations)? | Yes → Push for IEP; No → 504 may be sufficient |
| Is your child performing significantly below grade level despite classroom support? | Yes → IEP likely needed; No → 504 may be sufficient |
| Does your child need related services (speech, OT, counseling)? | Yes → IEP required; No → 504 may be sufficient |

**Why schools sometimes push 504 when a child needs an IEP**

Here's the reality: IEPs cost more. They require specialized staff, structured services, and extensive documentation. A 504 Plan requires less of all three. Some schools — not all, but some — suggest 504 Plans for children who might actually qualify for and benefit more from an IEP.

If the school proposes a 504 Plan and you believe your child needs an IEP, you have the right to request a formal evaluation for special education eligibility. That request triggers the full IDEA process, including the 60-day evaluation timeline and all its procedural protections. Chapter 3 covers exactly how to do this.

**The other direction: when an IEP may not be the goal**

Sometimes parents push for an IEP when a 504 Plan is genuinely the better fit. If your child's disability is manageable with accommodations — extended time, preferential seating, a quiet testing environment — a 504 Plan can often be obtained faster and with less conflict. It's not a lesser option; it's a different tool for a different level of need.

The goal is the right plan for your child, not the most paperwork. Use the framework above to make that determination — and we'll circle back to 504 Plans specifically in Chapter 7.

## Chapter 3: The Evaluation — How to Get Your Child Assessed (and What to Do If the School Says No)

Before any IEP or 504 Plan can be created, the school must evaluate your child. The evaluation determines whether your child has a disability and what their educational needs are. This chapter explains how to trigger that evaluation, what it must cover, and what to do if the school refuses.

**Child Find: the school's obligation to look**

Most parents don't know this, but schools are legally required to identify children who may have disabilities — even without a parent request. This obligation is called **Child Find**, and it's spelled out in IDEA at 20 U.S.C. § 1412(a)(3).

Child Find means that if your child's teacher notices significant academic or behavioral struggles, the school should be moving toward evaluation. It means that "wait and see" is not a legal strategy — it's a violation of the law when a child clearly shows signs of a disability.

If your child's school has been telling you to "wait and see" for more than a few months, and your child is still struggling, the school may already be in violation of its Child Find obligations. Documenting that timeline — with dates and what was said — matters.

**Requesting a written evaluation**

The most powerful thing you can do is submit a written evaluation request. When you put the request in writing, the 60-day clock starts.

IDEA requires that once a parent gives written consent to evaluate, the school must complete the evaluation within 60 calendar days (some states set shorter timelines — check your state's specific rules). [34 C.F.R. § 300.301](https://sites.ed.gov/idea/regs/b/d/300.301)

Here's what your written request should include:
- Your child's full name and grade
- A statement that you are requesting a comprehensive evaluation for special education eligibility under IDEA
- The specific areas of concern (reading, attention, behavior, speech, motor skills — whatever applies)
- A request for written confirmation of when the 60-day clock starts
- Your signature and date

Send this letter by email (so you have a timestamp) and also by certified mail to both the principal and the special education director. Certified mail creates a legal record that the letter was received.

**What the evaluation must cover**

The evaluation isn't just an IQ test. IDEA requires the evaluation to assess your child in all areas related to their suspected disability. [34 C.F.R. § 300.304] This may include:
- Academic achievement (reading, math, writing)
- Cognitive ability
- Language and communication skills
- Adaptive behavior (self-care, social skills)
- Emotional and behavioral functioning
- Motor skills (gross and fine motor)
- Vision and hearing (required by law)

You have the right to receive a copy of the evaluation report. You also have the right to review it before any IEP meeting is held — which is important, because the report will form the basis of every decision made at that meeting.

**What to do if the school refuses to evaluate**

When the school refuses to evaluate your child, they must give you a written notice explaining why. This document is called Prior Written Notice (PWN). We'll cover PWN in more detail in Chapter 5, but for now: any refusal to evaluate must be in writing.

If the school refuses and you disagree, you have two options:

1. **Request an Independent Educational Evaluation (IEE) at public expense.** Under 34 C.F.R. § 300.502, if you disagree with the school's evaluation (or their refusal to evaluate), you can request an IEE — an evaluation conducted by a qualified professional outside the school district. The district must either pay for the IEE or initiate a due process hearing to defend their decision. Most districts, facing that choice, will fund the IEE.

2. **File a state complaint.** You can file a complaint with your state's education agency (SEA) alleging a violation of IDEA. The state must investigate and respond within 60 days. State complaints are covered in detail in Chapter 6.

One parent in an online advocacy forum described requesting an IEE after her district refused to fully evaluate her daughter. The independent evaluation cost $2,800 — which the district paid. It identified needs the district's evaluation missed, and those findings formed the basis of an IEP that finally gave her daughter the support she needed. [COPAA parent survey](https://www.copaa.org)

**Timeline at a glance**

| Day | Event |
|-----|-------|
| Day 0 | Parent submits written evaluation request |
| ~Day 3–5 | School acknowledges receipt (they should, though it's not legally mandated) |
| ~Day 10–14 | School provides consent form for you to sign |
| Day 0 (of 60-day clock) | You return signed consent form |
| Day 60 | Evaluation must be complete; report delivered to you |
| After evaluation | IEP eligibility meeting must be held (no separate legal timeline, but typically within 30 days) |

## Chapter 4: Reading the IEP — What Every Section Actually Means

The IEP document can run 20 to 50 pages. Parents often sign it in the meeting, having skimmed it at best. This chapter breaks down every section so you know exactly what you're agreeing to — and what to push back on.

**Present Levels of Academic Achievement and Functional Performance (PLAAFP)**

This is the most important section of the IEP. The PLAAFP describes where your child is right now — academically and functionally. It should include specific data: reading at the 2nd-grade level, scoring at the 15th percentile on math fluency measures, completing 40% of written assignments.

Why it matters: every annual goal must trace back to the PLAAFP. If the PLAAFP says your child reads at a 2nd-grade level but all the goals are vague ("will improve reading skills"), the IEP is not legally compliant. Goals must be based on where the child actually is and describe measurable progress from that baseline.

What to look for:
- Does the PLAAFP include current performance data (test scores, work samples, observations)?
- Does it describe how the disability affects the child's participation in the general education curriculum?
- Are the present levels specific enough to serve as a baseline for measuring goals?

If the PLAAFP is vague or based on outdated data, note that before signing.

**Annual Goals**

IDEA requires annual goals to be measurable. [34 C.F.R. § 300.320(a)(2)] A legally compliant goal answers these questions:
- Who is performing? (Your child, by name)
- What will they do? (The specific observable behavior or skill)
- Under what conditions? (Given a passage at a 3rd-grade reading level; given a calculator; etc.)
- To what level of accuracy or frequency? (80% accuracy; 4 out of 5 opportunities; etc.)
- By when? (By the end of the school year; by the next IEP review)

**Bad goal:** "Emma will improve her reading skills."

**Well-written goal:** "Emma, given a 3rd-grade-level reading passage, will correctly identify the main idea and two supporting details with 80% accuracy across four consecutive measurement opportunities by June 2026."

If the goals in your child's IEP look like the first example, you can — and should — ask for them to be rewritten. Goals that aren't measurable can't be monitored, which means you can't tell if your child is making progress, and the school can't be held accountable.

**Services**

The services section is where the school commits to what it will actually do. Each service should include:
- The type of service (reading instruction, speech therapy, occupational therapy, etc.)
- The amount of service (minutes per session, sessions per week)
- The location (general education classroom, resource room, etc.)
- The duration (start and end dates)
- The provider (though specific names aren't required)

"60 minutes per week of specialized reading instruction in a small group setting" is a legal commitment. If that instruction isn't happening — if your child is pulled for services only occasionally, or services are being canceled — the school is violating the IEP.

Keep a log. When services are missed, note the date and why (if you know). In Chapter 8, we'll discuss how to document these gaps in a way that supports escalation if needed.

**Placement and Least Restrictive Environment (LRE)**

IDEA requires that children with disabilities be educated in the Least Restrictive Environment — meaning, to the maximum extent appropriate, alongside peers without disabilities. [34 C.F.R. § 300.114]

LRE does not mean all children must be in the general education classroom all day. It means the IEP team must consider the full continuum of placement options — from full inclusion to a self-contained special education classroom — and choose the option that is appropriate for the child's individual needs.

Schools sometimes use LRE to justify placing a child in a more restrictive environment ("he needs more support than the general classroom can provide") or, conversely, to avoid providing support ("she should be in the general classroom because that's least restrictive"). Both can be misapplications of the concept. The question is always: what placement meets this specific child's specific needs?

**Signatures: what you're actually signing**

There are typically multiple signature pages in an IEP. The most important distinction:

- **"Parent attended meeting"** — You are confirming you were present. This does not mean you agree with the IEP.
- **"Parent consents to services"** — This is the agreement that allows services to begin. You can consent to some parts of an IEP and not others.
- **"Parent agrees that goals were developed collaboratively"** — You are a required member of the IEP team.

You are allowed to take the IEP home before signing. Request a copy at the end of the meeting, take it home, review it carefully, and send your signed consent within a few days. The school may push back on this — don't let them. Your right to meaningfully review the document before agreeing to it is protected. If the school insists you must sign before leaving, write "Parent received copy and will review; signature does not constitute agreement with content" next to your signature.

## Chapter 5: The IEP Meeting — How to Walk In Prepared and Walk Out With What You Need

The IEP meeting is the most important event in your child's special education year. It's the moment when the team decides what services your child will receive, what goals they'll work toward, and where they'll be educated. Coming prepared is not optional.

**Who must be in the room**

IDEA defines the IEP team and requires that certain members be present at every meeting: [34 C.F.R. § 300.321]

- **The parents** (you)
- **At least one general education teacher** of the child (if the child is in general education)
- **At least one special education teacher or provider**
- **A school district representative** who is qualified to supervise special education and commit district resources
- **Someone who can interpret evaluation results** (often the same as the district representative)
- **The child**, when appropriate (typically in middle and high school)
- **Other individuals at the discretion of the parents or school** (specialists, advocates, therapists)

Schools may try to excuse a required team member from attending. They can only do so with your **written consent** in advance. If the special education coordinator says they can't make it and asks if you're okay proceeding — that's a request for your written consent. You are not required to give it. If you feel a required team member's absence would compromise the meeting, you can decline and request a rescheduled meeting.

**What to bring**

- Copies of your child's previous IEPs and evaluation reports
- Your own notes on what's working and what isn't (organized by subject and setting)
- Documentation of any services that weren't delivered (dates, missed sessions)
- A copy of the draft IEP, if the school sent one in advance
- A notepad for documenting what was said and agreed to
- A recording device, if your state permits it (most states allow one-party consent for recording; check your state's law before the meeting)
- An advocate, family member, or friend (you are legally allowed to bring anyone you choose)

**The right to bring support**

You may bring any person of your choosing to an IEP meeting. This can be a spouse or partner, a parent advocate, a special education attorney, a family friend who's a retired teacher, or anyone else you trust. The school cannot prohibit this. They may be surprised by it, or even express displeasure — that's not a legal objection. Simply state: "I've invited [name] to support me as a team member." [34 C.F.R. § 300.321(a)(6)]

**Scripted responses to the 8 most common school pushbacks**

These scripts work because they are calm, specific, and grounded in the law. You don't need to cite the exact regulation number. What matters is that you make clear you know your rights and are documenting the conversation.

**Pushback 1: "We don't have the resources for that service."**
Your response: "I understand resources are limited, but IDEA requires the district to provide services that are appropriate for my child's needs regardless of resource constraints. Can you document in the meeting notes that resources were cited as the reason for denying this service? I'll also need Prior Written Notice of this refusal."

**Pushback 2: "He's doing fine in the classroom."**
Your response: "The present levels in the IEP show he's performing below grade level in [specific area]. Can you help me understand how we're measuring 'doing fine' and what data supports that?"

**Pushback 3: "Let's revisit this at the next annual review."**
Your response: "I'd like to address this now. If the team decides not to change anything today, I'll need that documented as a team decision and reflected in the meeting notes."

**Pushback 4: "A 504 is enough for your child."**
Your response: "I've reviewed the eligibility criteria for an IEP and I believe my child may qualify. I'd like to formally request an evaluation for special education eligibility under IDEA. Can we start that process today?"

**Pushback 5: "We're going to try this for 30 days and see."**
Your response: "I'm open to a trial period, but I'd like that plan in writing with measurable benchmarks — what data will you collect, over what period, and what outcome would trigger moving forward with services?"

**Pushback 6: "He doesn't qualify."**
Your response: "I'd like to see the evaluation data that supports that determination in writing, and I'd like to understand the specific criteria that weren't met. I may want to request an Independent Educational Evaluation."

**Pushback 7: "You're overreacting."**
Your response: "I'm sure that's not what you mean to say, but I'd rather stay focused on the data. Let's look at [specific evaluation result or progress report] and talk about what it shows."

**Pushback 8: "We're already doing our best."**
Your response: "I appreciate the team's efforts. I'm here to work with you on what the data shows my child needs. Can we go back to the present levels and work through each goal?"

**When to request an adjournment**

If you feel pressured to sign something you don't understand, or if the meeting is moving too fast, or if new information is presented that you haven't had time to process — you can request an adjournment. Say: "I'd like to take the draft IEP home, review it, and come back to finalize it. Can we schedule a follow-up within the next two weeks?"

The school may push back. They may say they need a signature today. They don't. The IEP process is designed to allow parents time to review and respond meaningfully. If the school is applying significant pressure to sign in the meeting, that itself is a warning sign — and a reason to slow down, not speed up.

**After the meeting**

Within 24 hours, send a brief email to the special education coordinator summarizing what was agreed to: "Per today's meeting on [date], the team agreed to [specific services, goals changes, or action items]. Please confirm this matches your notes." This creates a written record of what was committed. It also signals, professionally, that you are documenting the process.

## Chapter 6: When the School Says No — How to Escalate Without Going to War

You've requested services. You've asked politely. You've cited the law in meetings. And the school still says no.

This chapter is about what comes next. Escalation doesn't have to mean litigation — and for most families, it never reaches that point. There is a ladder of options between "give up" and "hire a lawyer," and most disputes can be resolved somewhere in the middle.

**The escalation ladder**

Here are your options in order of formality and cost:

**Step 1: Written request to the principal or special education director**

Before escalating to a formal complaint, put your concern in writing to the school's leadership. Describe the specific service or action you believe the school is failing to provide, cite the IEP or legal requirement it violates, and request a written response within 10 business days. This step often resolves straightforward implementation problems (services not being delivered as written) without needing to go further.

**Step 2: State complaint**

Every state has a state education agency (SEA) that oversees IDEA compliance. You can file a complaint with the SEA alleging that the school district has violated IDEA. The SEA must investigate and issue a written decision within 60 calendar days. [34 C.F.R. § 300.153]

A state complaint is:
- Free to file
- Does not require an attorney (though one can help)
- Limited to violations of IDEA that occurred within the past year
- Focused on the school's compliance with the law, not on the "appropriate" level of services

State complaints are powerful for situations where the school is clearly not following the IEP (services being skipped, meeting timelines being violated, Prior Written Notice not provided).

**Step 3: Mediation**

IDEA requires every state to offer a mediation program. Mediation is a voluntary process where a trained, neutral mediator helps the parents and the school reach an agreement. [34 C.F.R. § 300.506]

Mediation is:
- Free (the school cannot charge parents for mediation)
- Voluntary for both parties (the school can decline)
- Confidential
- Faster than due process (typically resolves in weeks, not months)

Mediation works best when both parties genuinely want to reach an agreement but can't find common ground on their own. It is not effective when the school is acting in bad faith or when you need a formal legal ruling.

**Step 4: Due process**

A due process hearing is a formal legal proceeding before an impartial hearing officer. It is the "nuclear option" that parents and advocates refer to — and for good reason. Due process:
- Can take 6–18 months to resolve
- Costs families $15,000–$50,000 in legal fees in contested cases
- Strains the relationship with the school for years
- Can result in binding orders that the school must follow

Due process is worth considering when:
- The school has denied services your child clearly needs and has a legal right to
- You've documented violations over an extended period
- Lower escalation options have failed
- The stakes (years of lost services, significant academic harm) justify the cost

Before initiating due process, consult a special education attorney. COPAA (copaa.org) maintains a directory of qualified attorneys and advocates.

**The state complaint vs. mediation vs. due process comparison**

| | State Complaint | Mediation | Due Process |
|-|----------------|-----------|-------------|
| Cost | Free | Free | $15k–$50k+ |
| Time | 60 days | Weeks | 6–18 months |
| Attorney required | No | No | Recommended |
| School can decline | No | Yes | No |
| Result is binding | Yes (if violation found) | Yes (if agreement reached) | Yes |
| Best for | Compliance violations | Relationship-based disputes | Denied services, significant harm |

**Preserving the relationship**

One thing advocates consistently emphasize: separate the issue from the people. The staff at your child's school — the teachers, the special ed coordinator, even the principal — are often not the source of the problem. Budget decisions, district policies, and inadequate training are frequently at the root of what looks like individual bad behavior.

When you escalate formally, keep your tone factual and non-accusatory in all written communications. State the facts. Cite the requirements. Request specific actions. Avoid language that personalizes the conflict ("you have failed my child") in favor of language that focuses on compliance ("the services agreed to in the IEP on [date] have not been provided as written").

One parent who had been through due process described it this way: "I kept telling myself — my fight is with the system, not with the teacher. The teacher is in the room with my kid every day. That relationship matters." [r/specialed parent forum]

**What to do before you escalate**

Before moving up the ladder, make sure you:
1. Have tried to resolve it in writing with the school first
2. Have documented the specific violation with dates and details
3. Have requested and reviewed the relevant Prior Written Notice
4. Have consulted your state PTI center (parentcenterhub.org) — they provide free advocacy support

## Chapter 7: 504 Plans — A Shorter Path to Classroom Accommodations

Not every child who needs school support needs an IEP. For children whose disability can be managed with changes to how they experience the classroom — more time on tests, a quieter workspace, movement breaks, access to recorded lectures — a 504 Plan may be the right tool.

**Who qualifies**

Section 504 of the Rehabilitation Act uses a broad eligibility standard: any physical or mental impairment that substantially limits one or more major life activities. Learning, reading, concentrating, thinking, and communicating all count as major life activities.

Common conditions that qualify for a 504 Plan include:
- ADHD (inattentive, hyperactive, or combined presentation)
- Anxiety disorders
- Depression
- Dyslexia or other reading difficulties (when not severe enough to qualify for an IEP)
- Chronic illness (diabetes, asthma, Crohn's disease, seizure disorders)
- Physical disabilities that don't require specialized instruction

A key advantage of Section 504 eligibility: you do not need a formal medical diagnosis for the school to evaluate. The school must evaluate based on the functional impact of the condition, not just whether a doctor has labeled it.

**The 504 evaluation process**

The 504 evaluation is typically lighter than an IDEA evaluation. The school can use existing information — teacher observations, report cards, test scores, doctor's notes — to determine eligibility. There is no federal 60-day timeline for 504 evaluations (though some states set their own), but the school must act without "undue delay."

Submit your request for a 504 evaluation in writing, just as you would for an IDEA evaluation. Note the specific areas where your child struggles and how those struggles affect their ability to access the curriculum.

**What a 504 Plan must contain**

Unlike an IEP, there is no federal template for a 504 Plan document. But a legally defensible 504 Plan should include:
- The specific accommodations to be provided
- The name or role of the staff member responsible for each accommodation
- How accommodations will be communicated to all teachers
- The review schedule (504 Plans should be reviewed at least annually)

**Common 504 accommodations**

| Category | Examples |
|----------|---------|
| Time and pacing | Extended time (typically 1.5x or 2x), frequent breaks, reduced homework volume |
| Environment | Preferential seating (near the front, away from distractions), testing in a separate room, reduced noise |
| Materials and instruction | Access to audio recordings, digital textbooks, text-to-speech software, graphic organizers |
| Testing modifications | Oral responses allowed, multiple-choice instead of written answers, open-note tests |
| Medical | Access to water, scheduled snack breaks, access to nurse during the day, clear bathroom break permission |
| Behavioral | Check-in/check-out with a trusted adult, scheduled movement breaks, behavioral support plan |

**Enforcing a 504 Plan**

504 Plans are enforced differently than IEPs. While IEP enforcement runs through IDEA's due process system, 504 enforcement runs through the **Office for Civil Rights (OCR)** of the U.S. Department of Education.

Filing an OCR complaint is:
- Free
- Relatively fast (OCR aims to resolve complaints within 60–180 days)
- More accessible than due process — no attorney required

If the school is not implementing your child's 504 Plan, document the specific accommodations being missed (dates, which accommodations, what happened instead), attempt to resolve informally with the school first, and then file an OCR complaint at [ocrcomplaint.ed.gov](https://ocrcomplaint.ed.gov) if the problem continues.

**When to push for an IEP instead of a 504**

A 504 Plan is the right tool when accommodations are sufficient. But if your child needs specialized instruction — a different way of being taught, not just a different testing environment — a 504 Plan won't cover that. Signs that a 504 isn't enough:

- Your child is falling further behind despite accommodations being in place
- Your child needs a different curriculum, teaching method, or smaller group instruction
- Your child's needs require related services (speech therapy, occupational therapy, counseling)
- Your child is spending significant time outside the general classroom for academic reasons

If you see these signs, go back to Chapter 2 and the IEP evaluation process in Chapter 3.

## Chapter 8: Keeping Records, Writing Emails, and Creating a Paper Trail

The parent who knows the law but doesn't have documentation is like a carpenter without tools. Everything in special education — every promise made, every service denied, every meeting held — should have a paper trail. This chapter gives you the system.

**The everything-in-writing rule**

Verbal agreements don't exist in special education. If the principal tells you in the hallway that they'll add speech services starting next month, that's a conversation. Until it's in the IEP or a written follow-up, it has no legal weight.

The rule: anything that matters, put it in writing. Anything agreed to verbally, confirm it in writing within 24 hours.

**The follow-up email**

After any phone call or hallway conversation that involves a commitment from the school, send a brief email:

> "Hi [Name], thank you for speaking with me today about [child's name]. To confirm what we discussed: [specific agreement or decision]. Please let me know if I've misunderstood anything. I'm looking forward to [next step]."

This email does two things: it creates a written record of what was agreed to, and it gives the school the opportunity to correct any misunderstanding immediately. If they don't respond, the written record stands.

**What to keep — the full file**

Every parent of a child with an IEP or 504 Plan should maintain a file (physical or digital) containing:

- Every IEP document (original and all amendments)
- Every evaluation report
- Every Prior Written Notice received
- Every meeting notice and meeting attendance record
- Every report card and progress report
- Every email exchange with school staff
- Notes from every phone call (date, who called, what was said)
- Samples of your child's work (dated, especially if showing regression)
- Dates and reasons for any missed or canceled services

**Requesting your child's complete records**

Under the Family Educational Rights and Privacy Act (FERPA), you have the right to inspect and receive copies of your child's complete educational records. [20 U.S.C. § 1232g] The school must respond within 45 days.

Submit this request in writing to the school principal or records coordinator. A simple letter stating "I am requesting a complete copy of all educational records for [child's name], including but not limited to evaluations, IEPs, progress reports, teacher notes, and any other documents in [child's] educational file" is sufficient.

**When to use certified mail**

Send by certified mail (with return receipt) when:
- You are submitting a formal evaluation request
- You are requesting an IEE
- You are filing a formal complaint or notice of dispute
- The issue is significant enough that you want proof of delivery

One parent in an advocacy forum described the effect: "The moment I sent a certified letter citing IDEA, everything changed. The school started responding to my emails within 24 hours." [Wrightslaw community](https://www.wrightslaw.com) There is a reason for this: certified mail signals that you are treating the matter as a legal issue. Schools respond to that signal.

**Free resources, right now**

Every state in the US has a federally funded **Parent Training and Information (PTI) center**, required by IDEA. [20 U.S.C. § 1471] PTI centers provide:
- Free information and training for parents of children with disabilities
- One-on-one assistance navigating the IEP process
- Help preparing for meetings and understanding your rights
- State-specific guidance

Find your state's PTI center at [parentcenterhub.org](https://www.parentcenterhub.org).

**Other organizations that can help:**

- **COPAA (Council of Parent Attorneys and Advocates):** [copaa.org](https://www.copaa.org) — Directory of special education attorneys and advocates; advocacy resources; parent support
- **Wrightslaw Yellow Pages for Kids:** [yellowpagesforkids.com](https://www.yellowpagesforkids.com) — State-by-state directory of special education resources, advocates, and attorneys
- **PACER Center:** [pacer.org](https://www.pacer.org) — National parent advocacy center; clear, practical guides for parents
- **Understood.org:** [understood.org](https://www.understood.org) — Parent-facing resources on learning disabilities; strong on explaining evaluations and IEP basics

**When to hire a professional**

- **A parent advocate (non-attorney):** When you need someone experienced to accompany you to meetings or help you navigate the IEP process. Cost: $50–$200/hour. Find one at copaa.org.
- **A special education attorney:** When the school has denied significant services, when you're considering due process, or when the dispute involves potential litigation. Cost: $200–$500/hour. Find one at copaa.org or your state bar association.
- **You can do a lot of this yourself:** This playbook — and the free resources above — are enough to handle evaluations, annual IEP meetings, and most common disputes without professional help. Hire a professional when the stakes are high enough to justify the cost.

**A final word**

You are the most important person on your child's IEP team. Not the special education coordinator, not the school psychologist, not the principal. You.

You know your child in a way no professional ever will. You see them before school when they're anxious about the day. You see them after school when they're exhausted from working twice as hard as their peers. You are their advocate, their witness, and their expert.

The school system was built for the average child. Your child is not average — not in the dismissive sense, but in the literal sense: their needs fall outside the center of the distribution. Getting those needs met requires a parent who understands the process well enough to hold the system accountable.

Now you do.

Use the templates in the package. Find your state's PTI center this week. Go back to the chapter that fits where you are right now. And remember: the rules exist. They're on your side. You just have to know how to use them.
