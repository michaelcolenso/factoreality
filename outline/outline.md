## Section Structure

### 1. Introduction — Your Child Has Rights. Here's How to Use Them.
**Summary:** Opens with an emotional hook that validates the reader's frustration, then pivots to empowerment: the school system has legal rules it must follow, and this book teaches parents how to use them. Explains the difference between IDEA and Section 504, previews the book structure, and sets expectations for what the reader will walk away knowing.
**Word Count Allocation:** 900 words
**Key Points:**
- Your feelings of being steamrolled are valid — and common
- The school system has asymmetric information; this book levels the playing field
- Two laws govern your child's rights: IDEA (for IEPs) and Section 504 (for 504 Plans)
- How to use this book (sequential vs. reference)
- What this book will and won't do (it's a guide, not legal representation)
**Research Integration:** OSEP Fast Facts (7.5M children statistic); NCLD 1-in-5 statistic; audience pain points #1 and #4 (wait-and-see, cost of advocates)
**Examples/Data:** Opening with the "$4,200 for one meeting" parent quote to create immediate identification; 7.5 million children under IDEA statistic to normalize the reader's situation

### 2. IEP vs. 504: Which Plan Does Your Child Need?
**Summary:** Explains the practical difference between an IEP (more intensive, requires a disability category from IDEA's 13 eligible categories, provides specialized instruction) and a 504 Plan (broader eligibility, no disability category required, provides accommodations only). Gives a clear decision framework parents can use, along with the pros and cons of each.
**Word Count Allocation:** 1,200 words
**Key Points:**
- IDEA covers 13 specific disability categories; Section 504 covers any condition that substantially limits a major life activity
- IEP = specialized instruction + related services + the full procedural protection of IDEA
- 504 Plan = accommodations only (extended time, preferential seating, etc.) but faster and less bureaucratic to obtain
- Why some children with ADHD/anxiety need a 504, not an IEP (and vice versa)
- How to make the case for the right plan — including if the school has already decided
**Research Integration:** Section 504 source; OCR Dear Colleague Letter on ADHD (2016); IDEA 13 eligibility categories from 34 C.F.R. § 300.8; audience pain point on school defaulting to 504 to avoid IEP obligations
**Examples/Data:** Side-by-side comparison table (IEP vs. 504: eligibility, process, protections, what it provides); real example of a child who was given a 504 when they qualified for an IEP

---

### 3. The Evaluation: How to Get Your Child Assessed (and What to Do If the School Says No)
**Summary:** Walks through the Child Find obligation (schools must proactively identify children who may need services), how to submit a written evaluation request that starts the legal clock, what the 60-day timeline means, and what to do if the school refuses to evaluate. Includes the legal mechanism for getting an Independent Educational Evaluation (IEE) at public expense.
**Word Count Allocation:** 1,500 words
**Key Points:**
- Child Find: the school's obligation to identify and evaluate, even without parent request
- How to write a written evaluation request (this starts the 60-day clock)
- What the evaluation must cover (not just IQ; must assess all areas of suspected disability)
- What to do when the school refuses: written refusal triggers your rights (PWN + IEE option)
- Independent Educational Evaluation (IEE): how to request it, when the district must pay for it
**Research Integration:** IDEA Child Find obligation (20 U.S.C. § 1412(a)(3)); 60-day timeline (34 C.F.R. § 300.301); IEE right (34 C.F.R. § 300.502); OSEP Dear Colleague Letter (2023); audience pain point #1 (wait-and-see); audience pain point #6 (IEE cost savings)
**Examples/Data:** Sample wording for a written evaluation request; the $2,800 IEE savings parent quote; timeline diagram: Day 0 (written consent) → Day 60 (evaluation complete deadline)

### 4. Reading the IEP: What Every Section Actually Means
**Summary:** Decodes the standard IEP document section by section — present levels, annual goals, services, placement, and the pages most parents skip. Explains the difference between a well-written goal and a meaningless one, what "minutes per week" of service delivery actually obligates the school to provide, and which signatures matter.
**Word Count Allocation:** 1,800 words
**Key Points:**
- Present Levels of Academic Achievement and Functional Performance (PLAAFP): why this is the foundation of everything
- Annual Goals must be measurable: specific, observable, and tied to present levels
- The services table: what "60 minutes per week of reading support" legally means
- Placement: what Least Restrictive Environment (LRE) means and why "mainstreamed" doesn't always mean supported
- Signatures: parents are a member of the IEP team; signing "in attendance" ≠ signing "in agreement"
- What to do before signing: the right to take the draft home
**Research Integration:** IDEA IEP requirements (34 C.F.R. § 300.320); LRE requirements (34 C.F.R. § 300.114); audience pain points #2, #5 (vague goals, "nodding through the meeting"); PACER Center IEP guide
**Examples/Data:** Side-by-side: bad IEP goal vs. well-written measurable goal; annotated IEP section excerpt; the "Emma will improve her reading" parent quote

### 5. The IEP Meeting: How to Walk In Prepared and Walk Out With What You Need
**Summary:** A meeting preparation guide covering who must legally be present, what documents to bring, how to take notes, scripted responses to the 8 most common school pushbacks, and how to handle the meeting when you disagree. Explains the right to record and the right to adjourn.
**Word Count Allocation:** 2,000 words
**Key Points:**
- Who must be on the IEP team (and who the school may try to excuse without consent)
- What to bring: prior IEPs, evaluation reports, your own notes, a recorder if state law allows
- The right to bring an advocate, attorney, or support person — no permission required
- Scripted responses to: "We don't have the resources," "He's doing fine in class," "Let's revisit this next year," "You're overreacting," "We can try this for 30 days first," "A 504 is enough," "He doesn't qualify," "We're already doing our best"
- How to request an adjournment when you're pressured to sign on the spot
- What "prior written notice" means and how to request it if the school denies any service
**Research Integration:** IDEA team composition (34 C.F.R. § 300.321); PWN requirements (34 C.F.R. § 300.503); audience pain points #2, #3, #7 ("nodding through," "lack of resources," "certified letter changed everything"); audience glossary ("I didn't know I could bring someone")
**Examples/Data:** Meeting prep checklist (printable format); script table with pushback → response pairs; note-taking template

### 6. When the School Says No: How to Escalate Without Going to War
**Summary:** The escalation ladder from informal to formal: written complaint to the principal, state complaint to the state education agency (SEA), mediation, and due process hearing. Explains when each option is appropriate, what each costs (time and money), what outcomes are possible, and how to escalate without permanently damaging the relationship with the school.
**Word Count Allocation:** 1,800 words
**Key Points:**
- The escalation ladder: informal resolution → written complaint → state complaint → mediation → due process
- State complaint: low-cost, no lawyer required, state must investigate within 60 days
- Mediation: voluntary, neutral mediator, school must offer it free; faster than due process
- Due process: the full legal hearing; when it's worth it; what it costs (time, money, relationship)
- The "nuclear option" framing — and why due process is sometimes the only answer
- How to preserve the relationship: tone, documentation, and separating the fight from the person
**Research Integration:** IDEA dispute resolution (34 C.F.R. §§ 300.506–300.516); state complaint procedures (34 C.F.R. § 300.153); COPAA guide; audience pain point #7 ("certified letter changed everything"); audience glossary ("due process = nuclear option")
**Examples/Data:** Escalation ladder diagram; state complaint vs. mediation vs. due process comparison table; sample opening paragraph for a state complaint letter

### 7. 504 Plans: A Shorter Path to Classroom Accommodations
**Summary:** For children who don't qualify for an IEP but need support — ADHD, anxiety, chronic illness, depression — a 504 Plan is a faster, less bureaucratic path to legally enforceable accommodations. Explains eligibility, the (lighter) evaluation process, what a 504 Plan document must contain, and how to enforce it when accommodations aren't being implemented.
**Word Count Allocation:** 1,200 words
**Key Points:**
- Eligibility: any physical or mental impairment that substantially limits a major life activity (learning, reading, concentrating, communicating)
- Common qualifying conditions: ADHD, anxiety, depression, dyslexia (when not severe enough for IEP), chronic illness, seizure disorder
- The 504 evaluation process: less formal than IDEA; school can use existing information
- What a 504 Plan must include: specific accommodations, names of responsible staff, review schedule
- Enforcing a 504: Section 504 is enforced by the Office for Civil Rights (OCR) — a complaint to OCR is often faster than due process
- When to push for an IEP instead of accepting a 504
**Research Integration:** Section 504 statute; OCR Dear Colleague Letter on ADHD (2016); audience language ("504 vs. IEP" debate in parent groups); competitive gap (most guides don't cover 504 enforcement)
**Examples/Data:** Common 504 accommodations list (extended time, preferential seating, breaks, testing modifications, etc.); when a 504 is enough vs. when to push for IEP decision table; OCR complaint filing link

### 8. Keeping Records, Writing Emails, and Creating a Paper Trail
**Summary:** Practical guidance on the administrative side of advocacy: how to organize records, what to put in writing (and how to say it), the rule of certified mail, and how to create documentation that protects you in any future dispute. Includes a file organization system for every document a parent will accumulate.
**Word Count Allocation:** 1,100 words
**Key Points:**
- The everything-in-writing rule: verbal agreements aren't enforceable
- How to send a follow-up email after a phone call to create a written record
- What to keep: every IEP, evaluation, PWN, meeting notice, and school communication
- Organizing the file: recommended folder structure for your child's special education records
- How to request your child's complete educational records (FERPA right; must respond within 45 days)
- When to send certified mail (and what it signals to the school)
**Research Integration:** FERPA records request right (20 U.S.C. § 1232g); audience pain point #7 ("certified letter changed everything"); audience glossary ("Prior Written Notice"); practical advocacy from PACER and COPAA guides
**Examples/Data:** Recommended file folder structure; the "certified letter" effect on school behavior; sample after-meeting confirmation email template (previews the template pack)

### 9. Next Steps and Resources: What to Do After You Finish This Book
**Summary:** Action-oriented conclusion. Gives the reader a clear first step based on where they are in the IEP/504 process. Points to free legal resources, state-specific parent training and information (PTI) centers, and when to hire a professional advocate. Reinforces that parents are the most important person in the IEP team — not the school, not the specialists.
**Word Count Allocation:** 1,000 words
**Key Points:**
- Your first action: identify where you are in the process (pre-evaluation, post-evaluation, IEP annual review, enforcement issue) and go to the right chapter
- Free resources: every state has a federally funded Parent Training and Information (PTI) center — free advocacy help
- COPAA's "find an advocate" directory (copaa.org)
- Wrightslaw Yellow Pages for Kids (free state-by-state legal resources)
- When you need a lawyer vs. when you need an advocate vs. when you can do this yourself
- Final message: you are not alone, and knowing the rules is the most powerful thing you can do
**Research Integration:** PTI centers (IDEA mandate, 20 U.S.C. § 1471); COPAA directory; Wrightslaw Yellow Pages; PACER Center; all audience pain points (closing validation)
**Examples/Data:** Quick-reference table: "Where am I in the process?" → "Go to Chapter X"; PTI center locator link (parentcenterhub.org); reinforcement of the $4,200 advocate cost savings message

## Structural Notes

**Logical flow rationale:**
Chapters 1–2 orient the reader (what are these laws, which plan applies). Chapters 3–5 walk through the IEP process in chronological order: evaluation → document → meeting. Chapter 6 addresses what happens when that process fails. Chapter 7 covers the parallel track (504). Chapters 8–9 provide the operational and resource infrastructure for long-term advocacy.

**Cross-reference map:**
- Ch. 2 → Ch. 7 (IEP vs. 504 decision recapped in Ch. 7)
- Ch. 3 → Ch. 8 (evaluation request letter uses the email-writing framework from Ch. 8)
- Ch. 4 → Ch. 5 (IEP document understanding feeds directly into meeting preparation)
- Ch. 5 → Ch. 6 (meeting scripts reference escalation options covered in Ch. 6)
- Ch. 5 → Ch. 8 (meeting preparation references the template pack)
- Ch. 6 → Ch. 9 (escalation options reference the free legal resources in Ch. 9)

**Deliverable mapping:**
- Main ebook PDF: all 9 chapters
- Timeline cheat sheet: supplementary file; draws on legal timelines from Ch. 3 and Ch. 6
- Template pack (5 templates): supplementary file; templates referenced in Ch. 5 (meeting prep checklist, scripted responses), Ch. 8 (email templates, records request letter)
